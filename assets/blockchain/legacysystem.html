The Broken legacy System


The vast majority of organizations rely on computing systems to run their operations. The existing infrastructure has been develpoing for years and, as a result, many organizations find themselves relying on outdated legacy infrastructure. Maintaining these systems can be costly. In 2015, the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) found that 26 federal agencies spent a total of $60 billion on legacy technology investments. A recent poll of the private banking sector by Tenemos 80 percent of respondents agreed with the following statement: “Aging IT is the biggest threat to banks today.” It also found that maintaining legacy systems costs, on average, three-quarters of most IT budgets



How can we improve our systems?


The blockchain revolution has shown us the power of an immutable ledger, decentralized in control and encrypted for security. The powers of the world can return to the people in a trustless system, where no malicious actors can manipulate the immutable ledger.

In business, these transactions often occur under a contract with a defined set of rules. With blockchain technology, a set of rules – called a smart contract – is stored on the chain and executed automatically, adding the value of trust, security and efficiency for the parties involved.

A blockchain network can track orders, payments, accounts, production and much more. For example, if a company in the United States orders steel beams from a distributor in Russia through the blockchain, money can be held in escrow on the network until all contractual obligations are met – reassuring the distributor that they will be paid for their goods or services if they’re rendered. The blockchain will then record, in real time, the completion of milestones and contractual obligations so the buyer can track their order’s progress. Once the contract has been fulfilled and the order received (tracked with microchips communicating with the blockchain), the money held in escrow will be released to the seller. This record will be maintained on the ledger forever, and accessible to the network participants with undeniable transparency.

What are the benefits of blockchain technology?


So why do we need blockchains to manage these processes? Operations can often waste a lot of resources on duplicate record keeping and third-party validation, which can be vulnerable to fraud and cyber-attacks. Furthermore, transactions placed through a central authority can take a few days to settle, and only during the financial system’s business hours. With blockchains, transactions can be completed in seconds or minutes, even across borders. On the blockchain, transactions are protected by cryptographic algorithms, each block containing its own hash (a mathematical function that turns digital information into a string of numbers and letters). For a hacker to successfully manipulate this one block of information, they must first crack the hash code, then attempt to alter the data, and trick the network into believing it’s a legitimate transaction. To prove the validity of the transaction, the hacker must simultaneously control 51% of the network… Due to the size of most networks, the cost of accomplishing a successful hack is insurmountable.

Blockchains innovative automation and trustless system provides one of the biggest technological advancements to-date. The power of an immutable ledger will be the basis of business for the future.
